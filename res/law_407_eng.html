Notes on Lease Content

In principle, the formation of a lease agreement does not require a written contract. It only requires the mutual agreement of the parties on the essential elements of the lease, such as the use and benefit of the leased property and the payment of rent, and it can become effective without the delivery of the leased property.

The formation of a lease agreement does not necessarily require a written contract. As long as the parties have reached an agreement on the essential elements of the lease, such as the use and enjoyment of the leased property and the payment of rent, the lease will become effective, and the delivery of the leased property is not necessary. The landlord has the obligation to deliver the leased property to the tenant in accordance with the agreed-upon condition (Article 423 of the Civil Code), and the tenant has the obligation to pay the rent in accordance with the agreement (Article 439 of the Civil Code). Therefore, an oral agreement on a lease contract is valid. However, nowadays most people prefer to "contract" with the landlord (or tenant) when renting a property by signing a written lease agreement to be cautious. This can provide a written proof and reduce legal disputes.

So whether a lease agreement is made orally or in writing, it is valid as long as it meets the requirements, and this must be understood first. Secondly, whether the agreement is made orally or in writing, once the rental parties have agreed on the terms, the agreement becomes effective immediately. This is known as 'a word is worth a thousand pieces of gold.' At this time, it is necessary to pay attention to whether the terms of the lease agreement are in line with what is required. Otherwise, once the contract is established, both the landlord and the tenant are bound by it. Therefore, the following points should be noted when entering into the agreement:

❒ Identifying who the parties involved are

On the tenant's side, it is important to verify whether the landlord is the actual owner of the property or not. If the landlord is not the owner, then it is necessary to confirm whether the owner has authorized them to rent out the property on their behalf, for example, by requesting the landlord to provide a letter of authorization or power of attorney from the owner. This relationship needs to be clarified to avoid disputes where the real owner comes forward after the lease has been signed and claims that they did not authorize the rental, leading to the landlord demanding that the tenant vacate the premises. If the tenant is renting from a sublessor, they need to be aware of any restrictions on subleasing imposed by the main landlord. If the main landlord has such a restriction in the lease, they can terminate the lease with the sublessor and demand that the subtenant vacate the property (Civil Code, Article 443), which would result in the tenant's loss of rights.

On the landlord's side, it is important to verify the tenant's real identity (name) in order to confirm the recipient of future rent payments or when asserting rights under the contract, in order to avoid confusion and unnecessary trouble.

❒ Confirm the intended use and revenue status of the leased property

Most people have the experience of looking for rental advertisements along the street, which usually include terms like 'single rooms for rent', 'cozy suites for rent', or 'apartments for rent', and sometimes even specify details such as 'fully furnished', 'no cooking', or 'suitable for both residential and commercial purposes'. These indicate the state of the rental property that the landlord wishes to offer. Prospective tenants can choose what they want, but when actually entering into a lease agreement, they should clarify whether the property is for residential use only or can be used for business purposes, whether subleasing or sharing is allowed, whether furniture is included, and other details related to the rental property's status. This is because it involves what type of property the landlord should deliver to the tenant, and what type of property the tenant should return when the lease ends.

❒ Agreement on Rent and Security Deposit

As for the rent, in principle, the amount of rent can be freely agreed upon by the landlord and tenant. However, Article 97(1) of the Land Act provides that "the rent for urban houses shall not exceed ten percent of the declared total value of the land and its buildings." This is to limit the rent for houses and protect the usually more vulnerable tenants. As for the security deposit, when renting a house, it is customary in Taiwan to pay a security deposit in addition to the rent. However, Article 99 of the Land Act provides that "the security deposit for a lease shall not exceed two months' rent for the house. If the security deposit already paid exceeds the above limit, the lessee may offset the excess against the rent." The Land Act limits the amount of the security deposit to protect tenants, and both landlords and tenants should pay special attention to this when signing the contract.

In addition, according to the interpretation of the Supreme Court of our country, the payment of a security deposit belongs to a "contract for the delivery of things", which means that in order for the contract to be established, in addition to the agreement of both parties, there must be a transfer of "money". This is particularly important when the ownership of the property changes hands. If the transfer of the security deposit is not addressed between the old and new owners and the tenant, and if the original landlord cannot be located, the courts in our country tend to require the tenant to claim the security deposit from the original landlord. If the new landlord then demands payment of a security deposit from the tenant, the tenant will suffer a loss of the original security deposit. Therefore, it is important to remember to address the issue of the return of the security deposit among all three parties to avoid such losses.

❒ Who is responsible for the maintenance of the house

According to Article 429, Paragraph 1 of the Civil Code: "Unless the contract provides otherwise or there is a custom to the contrary, the lessor shall bear the cost of repairs to the leased property." In other words, in principle, when the rented property is damaged, such as a leaking roof or clogged water pipes, the landlord has an obligation to repair it for the tenant's use. However, the parties may also agree that the tenant (i.e. lessee) is responsible for the repair obligation, or even agree on which types of property damage are the landlord's responsibility to repair and which are the tenant's responsibility to repair. As long as the agreement is clear at the time of contracting, the property will not be left unrepaired in case of damage, and the landlord will not have to worry about poor maintenance of the property, while the tenant can live there comfortably and securely.

❒ Agreement on the rental period

The agreement on the rental period of the leased property depends on the free agreement between the landlord and the tenant, whether it is for a fixed period or not, subject to the limitation of not exceeding 20 years under Article 449 of the Civil Code. In addition, according to judicial practice, in the case of a lease without a fixed term, the lessor (i.e., landlord) is subject to strict restrictions under Article 100 of the Land Act if he/she wishes to terminate the lease, i.e., the lessor must meet one of the following conditions:

  1. When the landlord intends to reoccupy the property or rebuild it.
  2. Subleasing by the lessee in violation of Article 443(1) of the Civil Code.
  3. When the lessee fails to pay the rent for more than two months, except when the security deposit (rental deposit) is used to offset the amount owed.
  4. When the lessee violates laws and regulations in the use of the leased property.
  5. Violation of the lease agreement by the tenant.
  6. When the lessee damages the rented property or attached properties of the lessor and fails to make reasonable compensation.

According to Article 422 of the Civil Code, 'If the term of a lease of immovable property exceeds one year, it shall be made in writing. If it is not made in writing, it shall be deemed to be a lease without a fixed term.' Therefore, when entering into a lease agreement, the landlord should pay special attention to whether a term is to be specified in the contract. If the lease exceeds one year, a written contract must be signed and the special provisions of Article 100 of the Land Act should also be noted.

In addition, according to Article 425(2) of the Civil Code as amended in 1999, 'If an un-notarized lease of immovable property has a term not exceeding five years or has no fixed term, the rule of "sale does not terminate lease" shall not apply.'

  1. A lease agreement with a term of over one year that is not made in writing shall be deemed a lease agreement without a fixed term and is subject to the restrictions of Article 100 of the Land Law and Article 425(2) of the Civil Code.
  2. A lease contract with a term of over five years that is documented in writing does not have the problem of Article 100 of the Land Act, but it is still subject to the restriction of Article 425(2) of the Civil Code.

The above only provides a reference for the more important points to be noted when entering into a lease agreement. In specific cases, landlords and tenants may have some special agreements, such as penalty clauses, agreement on jurisdiction by a certain court, or even dispute resolution methods, etc. Of course, if they are explicitly stated in the contract, disputes can be prevented in advance.
