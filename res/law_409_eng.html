Lease-related Laws

The lease agreement is one of the named contracts under the Obligations section of the Civil Code. After the landlord and tenant enter into a lease agreement, both parties are bound by the terms of the agreement and cannot back out, except by mutual agreement to amend or terminate the contract. The termination, cancellation, and extension of the lease are all subject to legal regulations and cannot be unilaterally decided by either party. The differences among the three are as follows:

  1. Termination of lease: Refers to the situation where, after the rental contract has been established and become effective, the landlord or tenant does not wish to continue the contract for some reason and allows the contract to expire at a specific time. However, the lease prior to termination remains valid, and therefore the landlord and tenant are not required to restore the premises to their original condition.
  2. Termination of the lease agreement: Refers to the termination of the lease agreement at a specific point in time due to the unwillingness of the landlord or tenant to continue the lease agreement. The lease agreement before termination remains valid, and therefore the landlord and tenant do not need to restore the original condition.
  3. Extension of Lease: Refers to a lease agreement with a fixed term, in which the landlord and tenant agree to extend the lease period without changing the terms of the original lease after it expires. The original lease remains valid throughout the extension period.

❒ Termination of Lease Agreement

  • Termination by Landlord or Tenant:

    1. Tenancy at will: The so-called tenancy at will refers to three situations:
      • When a lease agreement is initially established without a predetermined term.
      • A lease of immovable property with a term exceeding one year that is not evidenced in writing (Article 422 of the Civil Code).
      • When the lease term expires, and the tenant continues to use and benefit from the leased property, and the landlord does not express their objection (Civil Code Article 451).

      The above three types of indefinite lease, originally under the provisions of the Civil Code, either the landlord or the tenant can terminate the lease at any time without any reason. However, based on the tenant's habit, it shall prevail (Article 450, Paragraph 2 of the Civil Code). For example, if it is customary to allow tenants to terminate the lease at any time, but not the landlord, the landlord still cannot terminate the contract at any time in order to protect the tenant. However, because the Land Law is a special law of the Civil Code, in these three situations, if the landlord wants to take back the property, he/she is still subject to the strict limitations of Article 100 of the Land Law. Furthermore, according to Article 425 of the Civil Code amended in 1999, indefinite leases should be notarized. Otherwise, the provision of "sale does not terminate lease" cannot be applied, and this should not be ignored.

      When terminating a lease, customary notice should be given. For example, if one month's notice is required according to custom, then one month's notice should be given. If two weeks' notice is customary, then two weeks' notice should be given. The length of the notice period is determined entirely by custom. However, the Civil Code imposes restrictions on the rental of real estate. If the rent is payable weekly, every half-month, or monthly, the landlord should take the last day of the week, half-month, or month, as the termination date of the contract, and should notify the tenant at least one week, half-month, or month in advance. For example, if the rent is payable monthly, the landlord should take the last day of the month as the termination date of the contract and should notify the tenant at least one month in advance.

    2. Fixed-term lease: A lease with a fixed term may not be terminated by either party without cause before the expiration of the term. However, the parties may agree in writing to allow one or both parties to terminate the lease before the expiration of the term. In such cases, the terminating party must provide advance notice as specified in Article 450-3 of the Civil Code. The relevant provision in Article 453 of the Civil Code states that 'If a party to a lease with a fixed term has the right to terminate the lease before the expiration of the term as provided for in the lease, the termination must be preceded by notice as provided for in paragraph 3 of Article 450.'

  • Termination by landlord only:

    Its situations include:

    1. When the lessee illegally continues to use and profit from the leased property after being prevented from doing so (Civil Code Article 438)
    2. When the lessee is late in paying the rent and fails to pay within the deadline even after being urged to do so (Article 440 of the Civil Code).
    3. Where the lessee, without right, subleases the leased property to others (Article 443 of the Civil Code).
    4. Where the lessee takes the property that has been seized, without the lessor's knowledge or against the lessor's objection (Article 447(2) of the Civil Code).

    When terminating the contract for the aforementioned reasons, there is no need for further notice, and the landlord may immediately notify the tenant of the termination of the contract.

  • Termination by the tenant only:

    Its situations include:

    1. In the case of a lease of a residence, when there are defects in the leased property that endanger safety or health (Civil Code Article 424).
    2. The landlord has an obligation to make repairs but fails to do so after being urged (Article 430 of the Civil Code), the tenant may terminate the contract.
    3. Where the remaining portion of the leased property cannot fulfill the purpose of the lease (Article 435, paragraph 2 of the Civil Code).
    4. When a third party asserts a right that prevents the use or enjoyment of the leased property (Article 436 of the Civil Code).
    5. When the lessee dies, although the lease agreement has a fixed term, the heirs may still terminate the contract, but they should notify in advance in accordance with the provisions of Article 450, paragraph 3 of the Civil Code.

    When terminating the lease for the aforementioned reasons, there is no need for further notice. The tenant may immediately notify the landlord of the termination of the contract.

❒ Termination of the Lease Agreement

The lease agreement is one of the 24 named contracts in the Civil Code, but the Civil Code does not have any special provisions regarding the termination of lease agreements. Therefore, except for any special agreements between the parties, the general provisions of the Civil Code should be applied. The following is a summary of the main points:

  • The reasons for the occurrence of the right to terminate the contract are:

    1. Reasons for the exercise of the right to terminate include: delay in payment. As leasing is a periodic act that must be performed within a certain period of time to achieve the purpose of the contract, Article 255 of the Civil Code stipulates that 'where, due to the nature of the contract or the expression of the parties, payment is not made within a certain period of time, and the purpose of the contract cannot be achieved, and the other party does not make payment within the time limit, the other party may terminate the contract without notification.' For example, the tenant and the landlord have signed a lease and paid the rent and deposit, and have agreed on the date of occupancy. Both parties have also agreed in the contract that the purpose of renting the house is to sell goods during the local temple fair held every 14 years. However, if the landlord is unable to deliver the house on time for some reason, the tenant may exercise the right to terminate the contract.
    2. Reason for Termination Due to Inability to Make Payment: Lease agreements are periodic in nature and require timely payments for the fulfillment of the contract. Article 255 of the Civil Code states that, "If one party fails to make the required payment within a specified time, which is necessary to achieve the purpose of the contract, and this is caused by the nature of the contract or the expression of intent by the parties, the other party may terminate the contract without the need for prior notice." For example, if a tenant and landlord have signed a lease agreement, with the tenant providing the rent and security deposit, and have agreed to the move-in date for the purpose of selling goods during the local temple fair held every fourteen years, but the landlord is unable to deliver the property on time due to unforeseen circumstances, the tenant may exercise their right to terminate the contract.
    3. Termination due to refusal to pay, such as the tenant's refusal to pay rent or the landlord's refusal to deliver the property for the tenant to reside in.
    4. Termination due to a change of circumstances, such as the situation where the house becomes unsuitable for living due to earthquake damage.

  • Methods of Exercising the Right of Termination:

    According to Article 258(1) of the Civil Code, "the exercise of the right to terminate shall be made by expressing the intention to the other party." The method of expressing such intention is not specified. In other words, whether it is done face-to-face, orally by telephone, or in writing is all acceptable. However, for the convenience of future evidence, it is recommended to use a registered letter with proof of delivery.

  • The Effect of Contract Termination:

    The effectiveness of the contract is retroactively eliminated by termination, and any unfulfilled obligations are naturally exempted. For obligations that have already been fulfilled, there is a problem of whether the undeserved benefits should be returned due to legal reasons. The legal effects after contract termination are mainly enforced in accordance with the provisions of Articles 259, 260, 261, 264, 265, 266, and 267 of the Civil Code, which primarily impose the obligation to restore the original status.

❒ Extension of Lease Agreement

As for the rent, in principle, the amount of rent can be freely agreed upon by the landlord and tenant. However, Article 97, Paragraph 1 of the Land Law provides that 'the rent for urban houses shall not exceed ten percent of the declared total value of the land and its buildings.' The restriction on the rent for houses is to protect tenants who are usually in a weaker position. Regarding the deposit, in addition to paying rent, it is customary for tenants in Taiwan to pay a deposit (referred to as a security deposit in the Land Law) when renting a property. However, Article 99 of the Land Law stipulates that 'the security deposit for a lease shall not exceed two months' total rent for the house. If the deposited security deposit exceeds the above limit, the tenant may use the excess amount to offset the rent.' The Land Law limits the amount of the deposit (security deposit) to protect tenants, and landlords and tenants should pay special attention to this when signing the contract.

In addition, according to the Supreme Court's opinion in our country, the payment of deposit for rent belongs to a "contract for the delivery of a thing". In other words, in addition to the agreement of both parties, there must be a delivery of "money" for the deposit for rent contract to be established. This is particularly important when the ownership of the property changes hands. If there is a transfer of property ownership and the three parties, including the new and old owners and the tenant, do not handle the transfer of the deposit for rent, the court in our country tends to ask the tenant to demand the return of the deposit from the original owner. If the original landlord cannot be found, and the new landlord asks the tenant to pay the deposit for rent, the tenant will suffer losses. Therefore, it is important to remember to reach a consensus among all three parties regarding the return of the deposit for rent to avoid suffering losses.

❒ Who is responsible for the maintenance of the house?

According to Article 429, Paragraph 1 of the Civil Code, "unless otherwise agreed by contract or customary practice, the lessor shall bear the cost of repairing the leased property." In other words, in principle, when a leased property is damaged, such as when the roof leaks or the pipes are clogged, the landlord is obliged to repair it for the benefit of the tenant's use. However, the parties may also agree that the tenant (i.e., lessee) is responsible for the repair obligation, or they may agree on which types of property damage are the landlord's responsibility to repair and which types are the tenant's responsibility to repair. As long as the agreement is clearly specified at the time of contract, there is no worry about no one repairing the damaged property, and the landlord can be assured that the property is well maintained, while the tenant can live comfortably and peacefully.

❒ Agreement on Lease Term

The agreement of lease term for rental properties depends on the free agreement of landlords and tenants, whether there is a fixed term and how long it is. However, it should be subject to the limitation of 20 years according to Article 449 of the Civil Code. In addition, according to judicial interpretation, in the case of an indefinite lease term, the lessor (i.e., landlord) is subject to strict limitations under Article 100 of the Land Law when terminating the lease. In other words, the lessor must meet one of the following conditions to terminate the lease:

  1. When the landlord wants to reclaim the property for personal use or rebuild it.
  2. When the tenant violates the provision of Article 443, Paragraph 1 of the Civil Code by subleasing the rental property to others.
  3. When the lessee fails to pay the rent for two or more months, except for the amount that is covered by the security deposit (rental deposit).
  4. When the lessee violates laws and regulations in the use of the leased property.
  5. Violation of the lease agreement by the tenant.
  6. When the lessee damages the leased property or attached belongings of the lessor without providing appropriate compensation.

According to the provision of Article 422, Civil Code, 'a lease agreement for immovable property with a term exceeding one year shall be in writing; if not, it shall be deemed a lease without a fixed term.' Therefore, the lessor (i.e. landlord) should pay special attention to whether the contract should specify a term when entering into the agreement. If the term exceeds one year, a written contract must be signed, and attention should also be paid to the special provisions of Article 100 of the Land Act.

Furthermore, according to the second paragraph of Article 425 of the Civil Code as amended in 1999, the "sale does not terminate the lease" rule does not apply to an un-notarized lease agreement for immovable property with a term not exceeding five years or without a fixed term.

  1. A lease agreement with a term exceeding one year that is not concluded in writing shall be deemed to be an indefinite-term lease, subject to the limitations under Article 100 of the Land Act and Article 425(2) of the Civil Code.
  2. A lease agreement with a term exceeding five years that is made in writing does not have the issue of Article 100 of the Land Act, but is still subject to the restrictions of Article 425, paragraph 2 of the Civil Code.

The above are the more important considerations when entering into a lease agreement, and are provided for reference only. In specific cases, landlords and tenants may have special agreements, such as penalty clauses, agreement on jurisdiction of a certain court, or even dispute resolution methods, etc. Of course, if these are clearly stated in the contract, disputes can be prevented in advance.
