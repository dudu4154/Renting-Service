Overview of Lease Legal Relationship

Is it necessary to have a written contract for the lease?

Renting is a type of contractual agreement. For most types of contracts, they can be established and become effective with just verbal agreement. For example, buying and selling is a type of contractual agreement, as well as buying newspapers or buying vegetables at the market. If we have to sign a contract for every purchase we make, then we would need to carry more contracts than cash when we go shopping or grocery shopping!

So basically, in the rental relationship, as long as the landlord and tenant agree on the house and rent, even if they do not sign a contract, both parties will be bound by the valid establishment of the rental relationship and cannot arbitrarily back out, otherwise they will bear legal responsibility. However, during the rental period, due to special provisions of the Civil Code, the presence or absence of a written contract will have a significant impact on the rights and obligations of both parties. This will be explained in detail in later chapters.

Is it necessary to notarize a lease agreement through a court?

Many landlords or tenants often request that the lease agreement be notarized, and the difference between notarized and non-notarized leases is often confusing. In principle, even if the lease agreement is not notarized, it can still be legally effective and does not necessarily have to be notarized to be legally effective. The most important purpose of notarizing a lease agreement is to protect the parties so that they can directly apply to the civil enforcement agency of the court for enforcement when the other party fails to perform the contract, without having to waste time and money on litigation. However, the amended Article 425 of the Civil Code in 1999 has made exceptions to the non-fixed term and fixed-term leases exceeding five years, as explained in detail on page three.

But the matters that can obtain enforceability through the notarization process are limited to:

  1. When the lease term expires and the tenant refuses to vacate, the landlord has the right to request the return of the property through legal means.
  2. The right to receive a certain amount of money or other substitutes (such as rice) or negotiable securities (such as a check) as the subject matter of the right.

For example, the landlord's right to demand rent from the tenant, the tenant's right to request the return of the deposit, or the landlord's right to claim compensation from the tenant for damage to the property, etc.

Any of the aforementioned rights must be stated in the notarized document and express the intention to accept compulsory enforcement, otherwise it still does not have the direct power of compulsory enforcement. Currently, the notary offices of various courts have well-printed lease notarization formats. As long as the various rights that are intended to be enforced directly are filled in the designated field and notarized, the effect can be achieved, which is considered convenient and can be utilized more.

Article 425 of the Civil Code: 'Sale does not terminate lease.'

More precisely, the implication of Article 425 of the Civil Code should be "the transfer of ownership does not terminate the lease." This means that if a lease agreement is established first and the tenant moves in to live when the landlord transfers ownership (i.e., sells the house to someone else), the tenant can continue to live in the house without any impact on their rights and interests. At this point, the tenant's landlord automatically changes to the new owner of the house (directly stipulated by law, without any action needed).

However, the revised Article 425 of the Civil Code enacted in 1999 imposes restrictions on the scope of application, excluding non-fixed-term lease agreements and fixed-term lease agreements exceeding five years that are not notarized by the court. In other words, if the lease agreement signed is a non-fixed-term lease agreement or a fixed-term lease agreement exceeding five years that is not notarized by the court, the tenant cannot use the reason for moving in and living in the property to oppose the new property owner. Therefore, in these two situations, the tenant will be in a disadvantageous position. This is probably a comprehensive consideration when amending the law to address the problem of false leases.
