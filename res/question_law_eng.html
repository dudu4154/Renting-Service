❏ Termination/Breach of Contract Issues

The issue of early termination of the contract also ranks among the top five disputes. In summary, the key to preventing disputes is to carefully review the contract terms before signing it.

➥ Sharing a rental house with classmates, before the end of the semester, one of the roommates expressed unwillingness to continue renting and moved out, but the landlord asked us to bear the full rent, is this reasonable?

First, it's best to confirm how your contract was signed. Did you appoint a representative to sign it on behalf of everyone, or did each individual sign with the landlord?

If you appointed a representative to sign the contract, you should make that clear. Otherwise, the landlord will only face the person who signed the contract (the "tenant" on the lease), and if they follow the contract and collect the entire rent, there is no illegality involved.

Therefore, it is recommended that if you and your roommates are renting a whole house together, do not be afraid of trouble and sign separate contracts with the landlord to protect yourself better. Landlords usually find it troublesome to write separate contracts for each tenant, so you can be flexible and list each tenant's name, scope of lease, and rent on one contract. If each person's scope is clearly defined, the landlord cannot require tenants to pay the full rent for the entire unit if one roommate moves out early, and avoid burdening you.

➥ Under what circumstances can a tenant terminate a lease early?

Only the tenant can terminate the lease under the following circumstances:

  1. When the leased property has defects that jeopardize safety and health (Article 424 of the Civil Code)
  2. When the lessor has an obligation to repair but fails to do so after being urged to (Article 430 of the Civil Code)
  3. When a part of the leased property is destroyed and the remaining portion cannot achieve the purpose of the lease (Article 435 of the Civil Code)
  4. When the property cannot be used or enjoyed due to a third party's claim of rights (Article 436 of the Civil Code)
  5. When the lessee dies, although the lease agreement has a fixed term, the heir can still terminate the contract, but notice should be given in advance in accordance with Article 452 and Article 450, Paragraph 3 of the Civil Code.

➥ Is it reasonable for the landlord to withhold the entire deposit if I want to move out before the lease expires?

If you want to move out before the end of the lease, it means you are breaking the contract. If there is a clause in the contract regarding compensation for early termination of the lease, you should follow the contract, but the compensation amounts in general contracts for rent often include clauses that not only withhold the entire deposit, but even state compensation amounts as high as five times the deposit. So tenants should be very careful when signing the contract.

If the terms of the contract are so harsh and you want to negotiate a lower compensation, please communicate with the landlord properly. After all, you did not object when you signed the contract.

If your contract does not include any termination clauses, it does not mean that you can come and go as you please. If the landlord does not agree with you leaving early, they can demand that you fulfill the contract (even if you do not live there, you still have to pay the rent).

➥ So what is a reasonable amount of compensation for early termination of a contract when entering into a lease agreement?

It is recommended that both parties agree to use an amount equivalent to one month's rent as the basis for the early termination compensation, which is a more reasonable amount.

➥ When moving out at the end of a lease, is it reasonable for the landlord to withhold the deposit until the water and electricity bills are settled?

When the lease expires and it's time to move out, there may be several expenses that cannot be settled immediately, such as water, electricity, gas, telephone bills, and even damages or compensation incurred during the rental period. Therefore, it is reasonable for the landlord to request to retain a portion of the deposit to offset these expenses. However, it is recommended that both parties try to estimate a reasonable price for the offset, and any remaining balance should be refunded first. Then, the landlord can settle with the tenant with receipts or invoices to avoid disputes.

➥ Can I refuse to move out when the landlord wants to sell the house and asks us to leave immediately?

Article 425 of the Civil Code stipulates that "If, after the delivery of the leased property, the lessor transfers its ownership to a third party, the lease contract shall continue to exist for the transferee. The preceding provision shall not apply to a lease contract for immovable property that has not been notarized and has a term of more than five years or an indefinite term." This is called "Sale does not terminate lease." In other words, the lease relationship continues to exist for the new owner, and the tenant can still use the property until the end of the lease period. The landlord asks you to move out first to create a simple environment for selling the property more smoothly. Therefore, if you are unwilling to move, you can invoke the provisions of the Civil Code mentioned above, but please also make sure that your deposit has been successfully transferred to the new owner if the landlord sells the property and inform the new owner of the lease to clarify rights and obligations.

If you are willing to cooperate with the landlord to move out, you can check the penalties for early termination of the lease in the lease agreement or negotiate with the landlord on related matters of relocation.

➥ Since the landlord refuses to sign a contract with me and only uses oral agreement to establish the lease, can I apply the principle of "buying and selling does not break the lease" when the house is going to be sold?

Article 425 of the Civil Code states that "if the lessor transfers the ownership of the leased property to a third party after the delivery of the property, the lease agreement continues to exist for the transferee. The preceding provision shall not apply to an un-notarized real estate lease contract with a term of over five years or an indefinite term." This is known as "sale does not terminate lease," which means that the lease relationship continues to exist for the new owner, and the tenant can continue to use the property until the end of the lease period. The landlord asks you to move out first because they hope to sell the property in a simpler environment. Therefore, if you are unwilling to move, you can invoke the aforementioned provision of the Civil Code, but also remind you that if the landlord still sells the property, please make sure that your deposit has been successfully transferred to the new owner, and remind the new owner of the lease to clarify the rights and obligations.

If you are willing to cooperate with the landlord to move out, you can check the penalties for early termination of the lease and negotiate with the landlord on related matters regarding moving out in the lease agreement.

Let's explain what an indefinite lease is: there are three situations of an indefinite lease:

  1. For a real estate lease contract without a document, if the term of the lease exceeds one year (Civil Code Article 422)
  2. When the lease agreement is initially concluded without a term being set.
  3. When the lease term expires, and the lessee still enjoys the use and benefits of the leased property, and the lessor does not express opposition (Civil Code Article 451).
Therefore, if you have not established any written agreement with the landlord, it represents an indefinite lease. For an un-notarized indefinite lease, it is usually not eligible for the protection of "sale does not terminate lease."
