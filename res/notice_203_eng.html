Tenancy Guidelines

For students who are renting a place for the first time and lack experience or familiarity with the local environment, the ideal situation is to first seek assistance from the school's accommodation and life counseling teams or to check the Truemom website to gain a preliminary understanding of the current housing situation and local market. Then, they can use the recommendations of senior students to learn about the condition of the room, the landlord, and the roommates to avoid encountering bad landlords or poor housing conditions.

Rule 1: How to find? (how to find a house)

The channels for students to find housing are as follows:

  1. School accommodation department and relevant rental housing groups established by the school.
  2. Red notices posted on public bulletin boards in the community.
  3. Recommendations from classmates, friends and relatives, and advertisements in newspapers.
  4. Rental housing websites and online BBS forums.

Since the red notices on public bulletin boards and newspapers are often filled with information from real estate agencies, the most effective way to find housing is still through recommendations from classmates. Generally, houses that are cheap and in good condition are less likely to be rented out through advertisements or red notices and are often circulated through niche channels such as recommendations from classmates or "generational transmission" of information between senior and junior students.

Many entire houses near schools are rented out to students on a long-term basis, and whenever senior students graduate and move out, other students automatically fill the vacancies through their network of classmates. As a result, these houses rarely enter the rental market and often circulate among a group of direct senior and junior students for several years. Some houses can even be passed down through generations and remain unchanged for more than a decade. Due to the long-term cooperation and trust established between this type of student tenants and landlords, the rent prices are often lower than the market average. Nowadays, it is popular among students to search for rental information on electronic bulletin boards on the internet or BBS forums. Since all major university computer centers have BBS forums, the old goods section or life information exchange section of the website has become a place for exchanging rental information. Moreover, this type of rental information is often posted by student landlords who are subletting their rooms, and the success rate of rental is high due to the specificity of the property.

It is worth noting that information on the internet circulates quickly. If the condition of the house is good and the rent is reasonable, it is usually quickly booked. Therefore, it is recommended that when students see a suitable house, they should immediately call the landlord to arrange a viewing and not consider contacting them via email or waiting a few days to contact them, in order to avoid being beaten to the punch by others.

However, because most of the information on the Internet is usually open to registration, it is easier for landlords to beautify the condition of the house or have unclear property rights (not the owner of the property or the sublessor). It is recommended that students ask clearly when contacting the landlord by phone to arrange a house visit, to avoid wasting time visiting a house and leaving disappointed.

Rule 2: How to save? (Ways to save money)

The executive director of the Tsuei Ma Ma Foundation, Lu Bing-yi, stated that due to the poorer economic status of students and the high rent prices in urban areas, smart students have already developed several practical rental strategies to save on expenses.

  • 1. Adopt the rental strategy of "trading time for space".
    If the area around the school belongs to the expensive rental market, then adopt a commuting approach to rent in the suburbs where the rental prices are lower. Taking the example of National Taiwan University students in Taipei City, because students do not face the same time pressures as working people, they rarely look for housing in the expensive Da'an District near the school. Instead, they rent in cheaper areas like Jingmei, Yonghe, Zhonghe, or even Xindian, and then commute using motorcycles or buses.

  • 2. Uses the MRT line as a consideration for renting a house.
    Students living near a MRT station have a wider range of rental location options to consider. After the MRT lines are in operation, Tamsui, Zhubei, Zhonghe, and Xindian have become new favorite rental locations for students.

  • 3. Jointly renting to reduce costs.
    Sharing one house with three rooms among three students is more cost-effective than renting three separate rooms. Moreover, they have access to shared living spaces such as the living room, kitchen, and bathroom. Sharing a house with like-minded individuals can also help avoid the inconvenience of living with strangers who have different living habits. However, some issues are worth noting. As an example, Lu Bing-yi points out that some students may impulsively pay a deposit when they first see a house they like, only to later discover a better option and lose the deposit because it is non-refundable according to the law. Additionally, some students may sign contracts without understanding them, only to later find themselves in disputes where their rights are not protected. Some landlords may also collect the contracts after they are signed and only return them upon termination of the lease, which is an unreasonable practice that some students may not realize they can refuse.

Rule 3: How to look? (General knowledge of basic house viewing)

When looking for housing, students usually consider the first criterion to be cheap rent, followed by the closer the better to the school. However, it is still important to remind everyone not to neglect their safety. After all, having personal safety guaranteed makes demanding living quality meaningful. Don't regret it just for the sake of temporarily cheap rent!

  • Electrical and gas safety:
    The number of tenants in the same household should not exceed five to avoid complicated entry and exit of members, and insufficient power supply which can cause danger of wire overheating due to excessive use of extension cords by tenants in different rooms. Check if the wiring is too old and the electrical load is appropriate. If there are black marks near the sockets or circuit breakers, it may indicate previous incidents of wire overheating. Make sure the location of the water heater or gas stove is cool and well-ventilated, and check for rust or damage at the joints of the gas pipeline to prevent gas leaks.

  • Security for theft prevention, door access control:
    Confirm whether the ground floor door can be tightly closed, and the bedroom door lock should be updated or installed with an internal bolt upon move-in. Check if there are sturdy iron bars with anti-theft function around the windows, especially for top-floor houses with public space additions, where door security should be given extra attention. Landlords usually prefer to rent their houses to students with a relatively simple socio-economic background. While the primary consideration for landlords when renting out properties is rental income, in today's society where public safety is deteriorating and conflicts between individuals are becoming increasingly clear, more and more landlords place significant emphasis on the background of potential tenants. Furthermore, renting a property is not a one-time transaction like buying a product. Landlords entrust their multi-million-dollar properties to tenants and have to ensure that rent is paid smoothly every month, so having simple background student tenants indeed gives landlords a sense of security. Landlords believe that even if students have problems, they can always turn to schools or parents for assistance, thus avoiding the tragic situation of an empty apartment. Generally speaking, as long as students show sincerity and politeness during the negotiation process, they are often accepted by landlords as tenants. Therefore, even if students have poor economic conditions and cannot afford high rent, if the landlord is willing to rent to them, they will usually receive some leniency in the rental price.

Rule 4: How to sign? (Points to note when signing a contract)

Confirm before paying a deposit to rent: If you are not satisfied with the condition of the house or the rent during the viewing process, you should avoid paying a deposit due to the landlord's or others' persuasion. Otherwise, if you regret it later, the deposit will be confiscated by the landlord. Before signing the contract, be sure to ask the landlord to provide identification and proof of ownership of the property to confirm that the landlord is the rightful owner of the property. If it is a sublet, you should pay attention to whether there are clauses prohibiting subletting and the original lease term to avoid future disputes. In addition to specifying the rent, lease term, and when the deposit will be returned in the contract, the responsibility for water and electricity fees, taxes, and property repairs should also be clearly stated in the contract. The condition of the furniture and equipment provided by the landlord and any promises of adding new equipment must be clearly stated in the contract. The rental contract templates available on the market generally provide more protection for the landlord, so it is recommended to read them carefully before signing and strive to delete unreasonable clauses or use the "TMM Version Rental Agreement" available for download on the TMM website. These are just a few precautions for students renting a house. For complete information on rental knowledge, such as rental contract templates and moving tips, please visit the TMM website.

Rule 5: Girls must read to rent a house.

When boys and girls live together.

For female students living outside the school, if they are staying on the same floor as male students, they should pay attention to their dress during weekdays and not wander around in their sleepwear in public areas. When hanging clothes, try to hang your personal items in your own room. If you need to hang them outside, cover them up and do not be too conspicuous. If possible, you can also consider hanging some male clothing to dispel any unnecessary imagination.

Camouflage Technique.

Even if all the students on the same floor are female, they should also learn from the "camouflage technique" of some insects. For example, deliberately placing a few pairs of men's shoes on the shoe rack outside the door, and remember to change their positions from time to time. You can also ask male classmates or friends to record the answering machine message.

Change the lock, install additional locks, and install iron bars on windows.

When deciding to rent a house, you can ask the landlord to change the lock or change it yourself, and request to install iron doors and windows, or security devices such as peepholes from the inside out. It is recommended to change the lock yourself and keep the old lock. If you move again, you can take your own lock with you and use it in the new place.

You can never be too careful with safety precautions.

Avoid letting others know that you are home alone. If someone visits, confirm their identity before deciding how to handle the situation, and develop the habit of turning off lights and closing windows when not in use. The distance between the rental house and the bus stop, lighting conditions, and whether there are businesses along the way are all factors that female students should consider when deciding whether to rent.

Prevent hidden camera filming by using a pinhole detector.

Some universities spend money on purchasing pinhole detectors, and female students who rent houses off-campus can also apply to the school for detection. However, generally only wireless pinholes can be detected, not wired ones, which are mostly found in shared accommodations with the landlord. Students can also check their homes for pinhole cameras that are smaller than grains of rice and sometimes emit light. If any suspicious light spots are found, they should be checked further. In fact, think about where others might hide a camera to spy on you, such as in the bathroom mirror, facing the shower or bathtub, furniture facing the bed, dressing area, or strange plants, tables, chairs, or sealed spaces above the wardrobe in the room or bathroom. If the landlord acts suspiciously, it is better to be safe than sorry and thoroughly check the room.
