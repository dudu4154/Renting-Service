Performance of Lease

Rent is the consideration for the use and enjoyment of the leased property, so the payment of rent is the most important obligation of the tenant. In addition, the agreed-upon rent is also an important factor in determining whether a lease agreement has been established. Therefore, in a lease agreement, the rent occupies a very important position and cannot be neglected.

Payment and Documentation of Rent

  • Matters related to rent, the following items should be noted whether they have been specified in the lease agreement:

    1. The amount of rent: The amount of rent is agreed upon by the parties (landlord, tenant), but to avoid future disputes between the parties regarding the amount of rent, it should be clearly stated in the lease agreement.
    2. Payment Method of Rent: The payment method of rent can usually be divided into two types: installment payment or lump sum payment. Installment payment is more commonly seen in lease agreements, where both parties agree to pay the rent on the 5th day of each month, for example. Lump sum payment is more commonly seen near schools, where rent is usually paid for a semester or academic year.
    3. Payment Period of Rent: After the amount and payment method of the rent have been agreed upon, don't forget to specify the payment period. If there is no agreement, it should be paid when the lease term ends according to custom. For installment payments, rent should be paid at the end of each period.

  • Effects of Late Rent Payment:

    The lessee shall pay the rent on the agreed date and shall not be delayed. The Civil Code also stipulates that late payment of rent may constitute a reason for the lessor to terminate the contract. However, please note that:

    1. Changes in rent due to changes in circumstances: As the leased property is immovable property and the lease term is indefinite, the rent may be increased or decreased if the value of the leased property has risen or fallen. The parties may apply to the court for a rent adjustment. The standard for rent adjustment is based on the value of the leased property (transaction value), and the addition of equipment to the leased property may also be a reason to request an increase in rent. However, if the lease is a fixed-term lease, the rent cannot be changed due to changes in circumstances, as a fixed-term lease implies an intention not to change the rent during the lease term.
    2. Reduction of rent due to use relationship: If the leased property is partially or completely unusable due to reasons beyond the tenant's control, such as natural disasters, third-party infringement, or the landlord's failure to repair the leased property, the tenant may request a reduction of rent or termination of the lease agreement for the part of the property that cannot be used. This is known as the principle of "partial loss" of the leased property, and the tenant may also seek compensation for damages.

  • Collection of Rent and Rental Receipts:

    The agreement regarding rent can include the method of collecting rent. The parties may agree in the lease agreement that the tenant shall pay the rent to the landlord on the agreed date, or the landlord may collect the rent from the tenant. It is common for the landlord to collect the rent from the tenant. After the tenant pays the rent, the tenant should request a receipt from the landlord to prove that the rent has been paid correctly.

    If someone other than the landlord collects the rent, the tenant may refuse to pay unless the rent collector can prove that they are authorized by the landlord to collect the rent. To avoid disputes, the tenant should pay the rent to the party who signed the lease agreement, i.e. the landlord, or at least obtain a receipt signed by the landlord.

  • Introduction to Security Deposit and Rent Payment:

    A rental deposit, also known as a security deposit, is used by a tenant to guarantee the payment of rent at the time a lease agreement is signed. Although the Civil Code does not explicitly regulate rental deposits, they are commonly used in society. The rental deposit serves as security for the tenant's payment of rent. If the tenant does not owe any rent when the lease ends, the landlord should return the rental deposit to the tenant. The scope of the rental deposit includes rent, interest on overdue rent, and damages. The amount of the rental deposit can be agreed upon by the parties, but if it exceeds two months' rent, the tenant can use the excess amount to offset the rent under the Land Law regulations.

Rights and obligations regarding the use of leased property

  • Rights and obligations of landlords

    1. At the time of delivering the rental property, it should be in a usable and well-maintained state: The landlord shall deliver the rental property to the tenant in a condition consistent with the agreed-upon use and benefits, for example, if the rental property is agreed to be a commercial building, delivering a residential property would not be consistent with the agreed-upon use, as the tenant would not be able to register their business. If the rental property experiences water leakage or requires repairs that prevent it from being used normally, even after the repairs are completed, the tenant may terminate the lease agreement.
    2. Obligation to repair the leased property: As a general rule, the landlord should be responsible for repairing the leased property. However, if there is a special agreement or customary practice that the landlord is not required to repair, such agreement or practice shall prevail. The tenant should give the landlord a reasonable time to make repairs. If the landlord fails to repair the property within this time frame, the tenant may terminate the lease agreement based on this reason, or the tenant may repair the property at his/her own expense and then request the landlord to reimburse the cost or deduct it from the rent. The term 'repair of the leased property' not only refers to the leased property itself, but also includes facilities such as stairs, elevators, water, electricity, and other amenities.
    3. Guarantee Liability for Defects in Rental Property: As a paid contract, the provisions on guarantee liability for defects in sales and purchases also apply to rental contracts. This can be divided into:
      • Guarantee of Rights Defects: The landlord shall guarantee that any third party shall not claim any rights to the tenant regarding the leased property. If a third party claims rights against the tenant and the tenant is unable to use the leased property, the tenant may request a reduction of rent or termination of the contract. This situation is most common in the case of subletting by a second landlord, but if the tenant knows about the defect of rights (i.e., subletting by a second landlord) at the time of signing the contract and there is no special agreement, the second landlord may be exempted from this guarantee obligation.
      • Guarantee of defects in leased property: As per the provisions of the Civil Code, regardless of the agreement in the lease contract, if the leased property poses a danger to the tenant's health or life safety, the tenant may terminate the contract. For example, if the tenant discovers that the leased property is made of sea sand or has radiation hazards.
    4. The obligation to pay all taxes and fees: In principle, the landlord shall bear the responsibility for paying all taxes and fees related to the leased property. However, if there is a different agreement in the contract, it should be followed accordingly.
    5. Reimbursement of beneficial expenses incurred by tenants: If tenants incur beneficial expenses that increase the value of the leased property and the landlord is aware of the situation but does not object, the landlord should reimburse the expenses when the lease ends, but only up to the current increase in value. Any fixtures installed by the tenant can be removed, but the leased property must be restored to its original condition. If the landlord objected to the beneficial expenses at the time, the tenant cannot request reimbursement. However, the tenant can still claim reimbursement according to the unjust enrichment provisions for any benefits that exist at the time of contract termination.
    6. The landlord has the right of retention for rent claims: If the tenant fails to pay rent, the landlord can retain the movable property of the tenant within the rental amount as security for the rent debt, such as refrigerators, televisions, etc., except for items that are prohibited from being seized.
    7. Claim for damages: The landlord's right to claim compensation from the tenant for damage to the leased property or for any damage suffered as a result of the lease agreement, as well as the tenant's right to claim reimbursement for expenses and the right to retrieve fixtures, will be extinguished if not exercised within two years.

  • The rights and obligations of the tenant:

    1. Duty to pay rent: As mentioned earlier, it will not be repeated here.
    2. Storage of the leased property:
      • Duty of custody of leased property: The tenant shall exercise the care of a good administrator in preserving the leased property. If the tenant breaches the duty of custody and causes damage or loss of the leased property, he/she shall be liable for compensation. However, the Civil Code provides a special provision in case the house is destroyed by fire, where the tenant shall not be liable for any compensation unless the fire was caused by his/her intentional or grossly negligent conduct. Additionally, the tenant shall use the leased property for profit according to the agreed method. In the absence of an agreed method, the method shall be determined according to the nature of the leased property. If the tenant violates this provision and continues to use the leased property despite the landlord's objection, the landlord may terminate the contract.
      • Duty to Notify of Hazards: During the lease term, if repairs are necessary for the rental property and should be borne by the landlord, or if it is necessary to install equipment to prevent harm or if a third party asserts rights over the rental property, the tenant shall notify the landlord immediately. However, if the landlord is already aware of such issues, this does not apply. If the tenant fails to make the aforementioned notification and causes the landlord to be unable to provide timely relief, the tenant shall compensate the landlord for the damages incurred.
      • Duty of Tolerance: The tenant must not refuse the necessary actions taken by the landlord to preserve the leased property.
    3. Duty of non-subletting: The tenant may not sublet the leased property to others without the landlord's consent. However, if the leased property is a house, and there is no agreement to the contrary, the tenant may sublet a part of it to others. If the tenant violates this provision by subletting the house to others, the landlord may terminate the contract. The tenant's subletting of the house to others does not terminate the lease relationship between the tenant and the landlord. The landlord is still liable for damages caused by the tenant's subtenant, i.e. the second tenant. The sublease relationship between the second tenant and the second landlord remains legally effective, but there may be issues regarding the defect of the right guarantee. If the landlord terminates the lease, the second tenant cannot use the property by relying on the sublease contract with the second landlord. The second tenant can only claim damages from the second landlord. However, if the landlord demands it, the second tenant still has to move out.
    4. Duty to return the leased property: Upon termination of the lease agreement, the tenant shall return the leased property in its original condition at the time of the lease. The term 'original condition' refers to the state of the property that would be expected after normal use. Otherwise, for parts that are subject to wear and tear, such as carpets, lighting fixtures, and bathroom facilities, would the tenant have to renovate everything to avoid liability? However, if the tenant has made holes, drilled or dug into the rented property for the purpose of installing an air conditioner or other equipment, the tenant should restore it to its original state. Failure to do so would result in the tenant being held liable for damages.
