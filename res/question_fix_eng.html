❏ Repair Issues

Repair and maintenance issues have always been the top concern for students renting apartments, so it is recommended that students make clear agreements in advance to avoid wasting time and energy in communication after the fact, which can even lead to the breakdown of the rental relationship.

➥ Who is responsible for repair and maintenance?

According to Article 429, Paragraph 1 of the Civil Code, the repair of the leased property, unless otherwise agreed in the contract or customary practice, is the responsibility of the landlord. In other words, in principle, when the leased property is damaged, the landlord has an obligation to repair it. However, the two parties can also agree separately on who is responsible for maintenance in the contract, and even specify in the contract which type of property damage is the landlord's responsibility and which is the tenant's responsibility. With such a clear agreement, disputes over repair issues in the future can be avoided.

➥ Equipment that was provided when I moved in was functioning normally, but it stopped working shortly after. Who is responsible for repairing it?

As stipulated in Article 429(1) of the Civil Code, the repair of leased property, unless otherwise agreed in the contract or customary practice, is the responsibility of the lessor. In other words, as a general rule, if a rented property is damaged when leased out, the landlord has an obligation to repair it. However, the two parties can also agree separately on who is responsible for the repairs, and even specify in the contract which types of property damage are the landlord's responsibility and which are the tenant's responsibility. With such clear agreement, disputes over repairs can be avoided in the future.

(Referring to Article 429 of the Civil Code) In principle, if a rented property is damaged or becomes unusable due to normal wear and tear during the tenant's use, such as leaking roof, clogged pipes, corroded lamp bases, etc., the landlord has an obligation to repair them for the tenant's use and occupancy.

According to the Civil Code, the scope of repairs should cover all rental items, ranging from the hardware structure of the house to the replacement of light bulbs, which should be the responsibility of the landlord. However, if even consumable items such as light bulbs need to be replaced by the landlord, it may cause some impact on the maintenance of the tenancy relationship.

Therefore, if equipment suddenly becomes unusable, the cause should be assessed first, such as whether it was caused by improper operation, whether it can be repaired by oneself with minor parts, or whether it was damaged due to aging equipment. After clarifying the cause and determining the responsibility, communication with the landlord should be easier to reach a consensus and solve the problem as soon as possible.

In any case, to avoid disputes over repair responsibilities, it is recommended that both parties specify the current condition of the equipment and the list of repair responsibilities in the contract when signing, in order to reduce the occurrence of repair disputes.

➥ A few months ago, I found some minor water leakage in the bathroom, which I didn't pay much attention to. However, the leakage has become more and more serious recently. I informed the landlord to come and repair it, but the landlord asked us to bear half of the cost. Is it reasonable?

It is the landlord's obligation to repair the leased property, but tenants also have a responsibility for the safekeeping of the leased property. Tenants who rent a property have the obligation to manage the leased property as a good custodian, which means not only maintaining the normal use of the property (not deliberately damaging or soiling the property, changing the structure of the property, etc.), but also notifying the landlord of any potential hazards.

When a tenant discovers that the property requires repairs, they should inform the landlord as soon as possible and agree with the landlord to repair it within a certain period of time (such as one week, two weeks, not using words like "as soon as possible" or "in the near future" which are uncertain) to prevent disasters from spreading and to avoid compensation for the landlord's losses. (Please refer to Article 430-432 of the Civil Code).

➥ I have informed the landlord that some equipment needs repair, but they have been delaying the process. What should I do?

Article 430 of the Civil Code: "During the continuance of the lease, if repairs are necessary on the leased property and should be borne by the lessor, the lessee may fix a reasonable period of time to urge the lessor to make the necessary repairs. If the lessor fails to make the repairs within the period, the lessee may terminate the contract, or make the repairs and claim reimbursement from the lessor, or deduct the expenses from the rent."

When encountering situations that require repairs, it is common to inform the landlord verbally first. However, if the landlord refuses or delays in handling the repairs, you can write a letter as evidence to inform the landlord to come and make the repairs (sample letter of evidence). If the landlord still does not respond, after the agreed period, the tenant can terminate the lease according to Article 430 of the Civil Code or make repairs by themselves. If the landlord agrees to let you handle the repairs by yourself, it is recommended that you estimate the cost before hiring someone, inform the landlord of the cost to avoid disputes arising from differences in the perception of the repair costs, causing the landlord to refuse to pay. After the repair is completed, all vouchers should be prepared to reimburse the landlord and deduct the expenses from the rent.

Advice: Repair issues have always been the most disputed issue. Remind tenants to clearly define responsibility and obligations before signing a contract. In case of problems, try to communicate and reach an agreement as much as possible. Remember not to use harsh words to avoid damaging the relationship between the parties, which may affect future interactions with each other.

➥ How should the repair responsibilities of both parties be included in the lease agreement?

Generally, repair responsibilities can be divided into several categories:

  1. Hardware structure of the property: such as partitions, walls, pillars, etc.
  2. Furniture and equipment: such as sofas, tables and chairs, televisions, refrigerators, etc.
  3. Consumable materials: such as light bulbs, lamp tubes, rubber inside water faucets, etc.

For the parts that require maintenance, it is best to list the equipment and parts involved, and then differentiate the responsibilities. If necessary, photos can be taken as attachments to the contract for documentation purposes.
